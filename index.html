Our Mission

Precision Rehabilitation Science for Neurological Disease

At the Nair Lab, we combine clinical physical therapy expertise with deep training in respiratory neurobiology, genetics, and molecular biology to advance the science of neurorehabilitation.

Biomarker Discovery

We identify functional, imaging, genetic, and molecular biomarkers that predict respiratory recovery trajectories in spinal cord injury, ALS, and Down syndrome. Our precision-medicine approach differentiates responders from non-responders before and during treatment.

Novel Therapeutic Interventions

We translate preclinical discoveries into human clinical trials, evaluating acute intermittent hypercapnic-hypoxia (HiCO₂-AIH) and transcutaneous spinal cord stimulation as neuroplasticity-based approaches to enhance respiratory motor function.

Respiratory Functional Testing

Using advanced pneumotachography, surface electromyography, transcranial magnetic stimulation, and 3T MRI neuroimaging, we characterize respiratory motor physiology and develop sensitive early-detection tools for neuromuscular disease progression.

Genetics & Epigenetics

We leverage single-nucleotide polymorphism genotyping, DNA methylation analysis, and extracellular vesicle profiling to uncover how genetic background (e.g., APOE3/4 variants) and epigenetic changes influence the degree of neuroplasticity achievable with rehabilitation.

Active & Recent Projects

Our research portfolio spans spinal cord injury rehabilitation, ALS biomarker development, and pediatric neurodevelopmental science in Down syndrome.

PI: Dr. Nair Active Craig H. Neilsen Foundation 07/2025 – 07/2027
$400,000

Non-Invasive Interventions and Biomarker Identification for Respiratory Recovery in Chronic Spinal Cord Injury

This project investigates whether transcutaneous spinal cord stimulation combined with acute intermittent hypercapnic-hypoxia (HiCO₂-AIH) can synergistically enhance respiratory motor plasticity in individuals with chronic cervical spinal cord injury. A central aim is to identify genetic and molecular biomarkers (including APOE genotype, DNA methylation patterns, and extracellular vesicle profiles) that predict which patients are most likely to respond to this combined non-invasive approach. The work directly addresses the heterogeneity in rehabilitation outcomes seen in the SCI population and moves toward a personalized neurorehabilitation model.

PI: Dr. Nair Active Foundation for Physical Therapy Research, Magistro Family Foundation Research Grant 2025
$175,000

Respiratory Neural Drive in Down Syndrome

Children and adults with Down syndrome (trisomy 21) face elevated risk for respiratory complications, yet the neural mechanisms underlying these deficits are poorly understood. This grant supports the first systematic characterization of respiratory neural drive in individuals with Down syndrome using advanced respiratory functional testing and neuroimaging at Jefferson. In collaboration with the Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia Trisomy 21 Program, Delaware County Down Syndrome Interest Group, and Jefferson pediatric pulmonology (Dr. Evertz), the team will develop sensitive, non-invasive markers of respiratory neural pathway integrity to guide future therapeutic intervention.

Co-Investigator Active Department of Defense, Congressionally Directed Medical Research Program, SCI Clinical Trial Award 09/2022 – 06/2026

Genetic Biomarkers of Acute Intermittent Hypoxia-Induced Respiratory Motor Plasticity in Chronic Spinal Cord Injury

Led by Dr. Emily Fox (University of Florida), this multisite clinical trial examines whether specific genetic variants predict the extent of respiratory motor plasticity elicited by acute intermittent hypoxia in people with chronic spinal cord injury. Dr. Nair contributes biomarker methodology and molecular analyses, drawing on his post-doctoral expertise in APOE genotyping and epigenetic profiling. The study establishes a critical evidentiary foundation for precision-based AIH rehabilitation protocols.

PI: Dr. Nair Completed Thomas Jefferson University Provost’s Pilot Clinical Research Award 07/2022 – 06/2023

Clinical and Imaging Biomarkers for Natural History of Disease Progression in Asymptomatic C9orf72 Carriers at Risk for ALS

This pilot study established feasibility of detecting early respiratory dysfunction in asymptomatic individuals carrying the C9orf72 expansion (the most common genetic cause of ALS) before clinical symptoms emerge. Using respiratory functional testing, MRI diffusion imaging, and molecular biospecimen analysis, the team demonstrated measurable differences in respiratory neural pathway integrity between C9orf72 carriers, symptomatic ALS patients, and healthy controls. Results provided the foundation for subsequent external funding applications.

PI: Dr. Nair Completed Thomas Jefferson University JCRS Dean’s Research Award 07/2024 – 06/2025

Optimization of Acute Intermittent Hypoxia Protocol Combined with Transcutaneous Spinal Stimulation to Improve Respiratory Neuroplasticity in Chronic Spinal Cord Injury

This award supported protocol development and pilot data collection for the combined HiCO₂-AIH plus transcutaneous spinal cord stimulation approach. Work included optimization of stimulation parameters, characterization of cardiorespiratory safety profiles, and refinement of outcome measures for subsequent larger-scale funded trials.

Principal Investigator

Jayakrishnan Nair, PT, MSPT, PhD

Dr. Nair is a physical therapist and clinician-scientist whose research focuses on developing functional, imaging, genetic, and molecular biomarkers to predict recovery outcomes in patients with neurological injuries and diseases affecting the control of breathing. At Thomas Jefferson University, his work centers on spinal cord injury, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, and Down syndrome.

He completed his PhD in Rehabilitation Sciences with a concentration in respiratory neurobiology at the University of Florida, followed by two post-doctoral fellowships: the first in genetics and molecular biology (Barry Byrne Gene Therapy Lab, Department of Pediatrics), and the second in respiratory physiology and human spinal cord injury rehabilitation (Gordon Mitchell Lab and Emily Fox Lab, Department of Physical Therapy). This breadth of training uniquely positions him to bridge cellular and molecular biology with evidence-based clinical rehabilitation.

Since joining Jefferson in 2022 as an Assistant Professor, Dr. Nair has established an active translational research laboratory, secured competitive extramural funding, and built a highly collaborative interdisciplinary team spanning neuroimaging, neurology, molecular neuroscience, and pediatric pulmonology. He is also a primary instructor in Jefferson’s Doctor of Physical Therapy program, teaching Evidence-Based Practice I and II.

His research has been recognized with multiple awards from the American Physiological Society and he holds active peer reviewer roles for journals including Physical Therapy & Rehabilitation Journal, Physiological Reports, Spinal Cord, and Neurotrauma.
